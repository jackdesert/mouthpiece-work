Here's something interesting.   The harder you blow, the more the reed closes and the less air enters the mouthpiece.  The only reason the reed vibrates at all, is because the pressure inside the mouthpiece forces the reed open, letting air in.  So it would appear as if the efficiency with which air pressure manipulates the reed would be more important than how air is channeled into the mouthpiece.  Why all the emphasis on airflow?

FROM: lancelotburt (MartinMods)
SUBJECT: Re: Airflow
"I think there is emphasis on airflow because it has been a useful teaching aid."

As with other similar, commonly employed teaching aids (eg., blowing fast air improves tone at p dynamic levels), I grew up hearing and using them as much as anyone else, and they work, to a point.  Nothing has improved every aspect of my playing and even more-so my work on mouthpieces, as much or as rapidly as having an accurate child's "picture book" grasp of what is actually going on.  This is the information age and the Truth really will set you free.  

It's a good teaching aid for beginners, but after a point it will only hold you back.

"But I have been puzzled by graphs that show the reed tip gradually 
closing off more as you blow harder.  The graph shows that airflow is 
decreasing as you blow harder but the sensation is that you are using 
more air."

The graph is always labeled with flow (u) on the vertical axis and pressure (P-p) on the horizontal axis.  "P" = blowing pressure in the mouth.  "p" = pressure inside the mouthpiece.  On a scale of 1 - 10 of blowing pressure, if we blow steadily at 10 and the pressure inside the mouthpiece is 0, the graph shows the air flow is 0, which it is, as the reed closes and stays closed.  If we have + pressure of 5 inside the mouthpiece as the - pressure wave from the open tone hole/bell reflects at the reed with inversion, the pressure on the reed is now 10 - 5 = 5, so the reed is allowed to open to that degree (dependent upon reed strength) and a certain amount of that force 10 blown air, enters the mouthpiece.  For any given blowing pressure, the higher the mouthpiece pressure on the one side of the reed, the more it counteracts the blowing pressure on the other side of the reed, the wider the reed opens, and the more air goes into the mouthpiece.

So, no matter how hard you are blowing, the only reason any air enters the mouthpiece is because there is a pressure build-up on the other side of the reed that allows it to open.  From the mouthpiece design standpoint, how accurately and to what intensity, changes in pressure occur directly under the reed at the speed of sound  - how efficiently that pressure controls the reed - are of more importance than how the little puffs of air that enter the mouthpiece traveling at.......what?   30 mph, flow into the chamber, throat, and bore.

"So since there is a strong 3 parameter relationship going on, is there a better illustration published that shows how p varies?"

p is the standing wave.  It's a feedback loop.  P generates but also wants to shut off the U that causes reed motion.  p is the initial reed motion pressure feeding back upon itself after 2 transits of the air column (+ the mysterious Helmholtz thing it does in the chamber).  The more it feeds back without distortion and signal loss, the more it influences the reed's motion, enabling more U.

This efficiency (for a free-blowing setup) depends mostly on the design and quality of the facing curve and reed.  Next, I think the baffle design near the tip is important.  Then the wall shape at the base of the window.  I would say throat geometry and sidewalls are forth on my list.  

Not all players want a really free-blowing mouthpiece either.  Most are willing to trade off blowing efficiency for gains in other areas like a darker more spread tone, if that is their sound concept.

I agree that air flow considerations should rank a lot lower than many mouthpiece makers and players think.

I think of optimal reed and facing aspects as an assumed prerequisite, just as I assume the horn has correct venting and leak-free padding.  I use a model reed, establish my preferred facing for my model embouchure, and then go about adjusting chamber parameters to suite, whether the objective is a fee-blowing setup or something with slightly less efficiency, giving the player more tonal control. In any case, the focus is on pressure and pressure related particle flow, rather than the flow of blown air into the mouthpiece.

The graph is always labeled with flow (u) on the vertical axis and pressure (P-p) on the horizontal axis.  "P" = blowing pressure in the mouth.  "p" = pressure inside the mouthpiece.  On a scale of 1 - 10 of blowing pressure, if we blow steadily at 10 and the pressure inside the mouthpiece is 0, the graph shows the air flow is 0, which it is, as the reed closes and stays closed.  If we have + pressure of 5 inside the mouthpiece as the - pressure wave from the open tone hole/bell reflects at the reed with inversion, the pressure on the reed is now 10 - 5 = 5, so the reed is allowed to open to that degree (dependent upon reed strength) and a certain amount of that force 10 blown air, enters the mouthpiece.  For any given blowing pressure, the higher the mouthpiece pressure on the one side of the reed, the more it counteracts the blowing pressure on the other side of the reed, the wider the reed opens, and the more
 air goes into the mouthpiece.

Player A gets a characteristic saxophone tone and is enamored with what he considers his ability to accent, and begin and end phrases "neatly" by manipulating his throat to control the air flow.  

Player B learned years ago, that such
 "throat playing" is actually indicative of a total lack of embouchure/tonal control at
 anything other than a f - ff dynamic level, and like all other serious saxophone players, before and since, strove to eliminate any hint of such throat manipulation tendencies from his playing.  In doing so, a whole world of professional playing experiences became his, which Player A would never have, as, there isn't
 one thing that will get you tossed out of any professional playing 
opportunity faster (other than not showing up) than, "using your throat".

So, what works fine for Player A for one reason in his circumstance, Player B will avoid at all costs for another in his.  The former will have nothing to say regarding tone that the latter could possibly find of interest.  The latter can say nothing on tone that the former is capable of understanding.  What a mess!  Where is Ego doing the talking?

Regardless of what our player's artistic requirements are, the mouthpiece facing has to do 3 things.  

1. provide an air-tight seal against the reed
2. provide a springy feel/response so the player can easily control the frequency of the reed's resonance over the range required for correct playing.
3. allow the reed to roll up the facing and close in a manner that is energy efficient and results in musically interesting tone qualities. 
 

In #3, I differentiate between mechanical resistance (stress/flexing) and acoustical resistance.  I use the facing to control mechanical resistance - the way the model reed responds to P (air pressure in the mouth) in the absence of p.  I use very, very fine baffle adjustments to control acoustical resistance (once the other main considerations have been set) - the way the model reed responds to p in the presence of P, or P - p. That gives me the mouthpiece results I want for my playing demands.  

I work with machine tools and hand scrape flat surfaces to .0005" accuracy (in 12").   While I find mouthpiece facings interesting, I don't consider them mysterious at all.

 always labeled with flow (u) on the vertical axis and pressure (P-p) on the horizontal axis.  "P" = blowing pressure in the mouth.  "p" = pressure inside the mouthpiece.  On a scale of 1 - 10 of blowing pressure, if we blow steadily at 10 and the pressure inside the mouthpiece is 0, the graph shows the air flow is 0, which it is, as the reed closes and stays closed.  If we have + pressure of 5 inside the mouthpiece as the - pressure wave from the open tone hole/bell reflects at the reed with inversion, the pressure on the reed is now 10 - 5 = 5, so the reed is allowed to open to that degree (dependent upon reed strength) and a certain amount of that force 10 blown air, enters the mouthpiece.  For any given blowing pressure, the higher the mouthpiece pressure on the one side of the reed, the more it counteracts the blowing pressure on the other side of the reed, the wider the reed opens, and the more air goes into the
 mouthpiece.

So, no matter how hard you are blowing, the only reason any air enters the mouthpiece is because there is a pressure build-up on the other side of the reed that allows it to open.  From the mouthpiece design standpoint, how accurately and to what intensity, changes in pressure occur directly under the reed at the speed of sound  - how efficiently that pressure controls the reed - are of more importance than how the little puffs of air that enter the mouthpiece traveling at.......what?   30 mph, flow into the chamber, throat, and bore.
