Actually Readers, the circular objects do not have to be made from wood.  Plastic will work.  Soft rubber cylinders would be better, since they would form to the shape of the neck.  That's what I use.  

Going back to basic geometry, the rigid straight line segments will intersect the center-line arc at one point only.  All other points will be outside the arc, resulting in measurements in considerable excess of the actual center-line length.  The diameter of the "bead" hole will normalize the error to a very small degree, but not enough to make the results accurate.  The larger the bead, the more excessive the error, and one would use the largest beads in the bent section of the neck.

FROM: jbtsax (John)

Actually Lance that wouldn't work.  On the top of the neck you have the
octave pip, the octave key guide cradle, the octave key hinge post
assembly that are in the way of getting an accurate measurement.  On the
bottom of most necks you have the brace that extends out from the
surface.  For being a stickler for accuracy, I'm surprised you
overlooked these common attributes of a sax neck.

Waves in the open air propagate in straight lines from their source .  
Standing waves in an enclosed tube follow the contours of the bends in
the tube.  A sound wave is not at all like the single curved line with
humps and valleys that represents a sine wave in the common
illustrations.  A sound wave in an enclosed tube  is also not like a
billiard ball that strikes a hard surface and then bounces off at an
equal but opposite angle.

The bottom line is that the sounding length or acoustic length of a wind
instrument is the geometric length of its tubing, which means the length
of an imaginary string that runs through the center, plus adjustments
for closed tone holes,  sharp bends in the tubing, and end corrections. 
Measuring the path of the soundwave through a neck as though it were a
billiard ball going straight till it hits a wall is just plain silly.

On the top of the neck you have the octave pip, the octave key guide cradle, the octave key hinge post assembly that are in the way of getting an accurate measurement.  On the bottom of most necks you have the brace that extends out from the surface.  For being a stickler for accuracy, I'm surprised you overlooked these common attributes of a sax neck.

  Waves in the open air propagate in straight lines from their source .   Standing waves in an enclosed tube follow the contours of the bends in the tube.  A sound wave is not at all like the single curved line with humps and valleys that represents a sine wave in the common illustrations.  A sound wave in an enclosed tube  is also not like a billiard ball that strikes a hard surface and then bounces off at an equal but opposite angle.  

  The bottom line is that the sounding length or acoustic length of a wind instrument is the geometric length of its tubing, which means the length of an imaginary string that runs through the center, plus adjustments for closed tone holes,  sharp bends in the tubing, and end corrections.  Measuring the path of the soundwave through a neck as though it were a billiard ball going straight till it hits a wall is just plain silly.

Here are some definitions of basic terms that may be helpful in this
discussion

Reed compliance

The elasticity of the reed that allows it to vibrate.  Compliance
<http://www.phys.unsw.edu.au/jw/saxacoustics.html#compliance>   is the
opposite of "stiffness".  A soft reed has more "compliance" than a hard
reed and therefore moves farther.  This wider path of motion increases
the effective volume inside the mouthpiece and also lowers the pitch
especially on higher notes.  Joe Wolfe wrote in a private communication
that the stiffness of a tenor reed can make as much as 2 ml difference
in the "effective volume" of the mouthpiece.

Reed dampening

In actual playing due to the curvature of the lay, the pressure of the
players lip and its position on the reed have a direct  effect upon the
compliance of the saxophone reed
<http://www.phys.unsw.edu.au/jw/saxacoustics.html#reed>  .  As one bites
harder the effective length of the reed (the portion allowed to vibrate)
becomes shorter and therefore the reed becomes stiffer.   Under actual
playing conditions the dampening effect of the lip on the reed in
conjunction with the reed's natural compliance (degree of stiffness)
together determine the amount of volume added to the geometric volume of
the mouthpiece to produce its "effective" or "equivalent" volume.

Sound waves

Sound <http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound>   is a traveling wave which
is an oscillation of pressure (not frequency)  transmitted through a
solid, liquid or gas composed of frequencies within the range of hearing
and of a level sufficiently strong to be heard.

Modulation

In electronics modulation <http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulation>   is
the process of varying one or more properties of high frequency periodic
waveform, called the carrier signal, with respect to a modulating
signal.   In music "modulation" can also refer to changing key.

Frequency Modulation

In telecommunications,  frequency modulation
<http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_modulation>   (FM) conveys
information over a carrier wave by varying its frequency.  The only time
one would find "frequency modulation" in a saxophone tone would be when
a lip vibrato is used.

Frs

As used by Benade in his text "Fundamentals of Musical Acoustics"  the
term Frs refers specifically to the Frequency of the (oboe) reed on its
staple.  The equivalent on the bassoon is the frequency of the reed on
its bocal, and on the saxophone, the frequency of the mouthpiece on its
neck.   The term Frs coined by Benade should not be confused with the
natural resonant frequency of the mouthpiece, oboe reed, or bassoon reed
alone.

It is hoped these definitions clear up any misunderstandings on this
topic.

FROM: lancelotburt (MartinMods)
SUBJECT: Re: substitution volume and frs sweet spot
On a saxophone, the "reed closed"/"reed open" ratio becomes larger, the shorter the sounding tube length becomes.  When the reed closes, the tube becomes a closed resonating chamber with a fixed volume and stays that way for the length of time it takes the rarefaction to travel 1/2 the mouthpiece + constriction resonance (frs) wavelength (actual - rarefaction from reed to constriction, partial reflection as compression wave back to reed.  theoretical - truncation to apex and back).  As all fn resonances share the mouthpiece compression anti-node, all fn resonances are affected, for that
 length of time and on
 that related portion of
 their wavelength. The
 regime will balance itself
 accordingly. 
 

If we could play a long tone, and gradually reduce the mouthpiece volume, the first place we would notice the effects of too little volume, would be during this "reed closed" phase.  The regime would become increasingly unbalanced as the resonance peaks shifted further from integral, until it would not form at all during that period, perhaps stopping tone production completely - the reed closes and won't open again.  

If we could then gradually increase the mouthpiece volume, past that of the ideal, the same resonance shift and regime degradation would first happen during the "reed open" phase.

So, we have a functional mouthpiece volume range that must be satisfied at both extremes, for optimal results.

I'm inclined to think that, having identical volume and length, a mouthpiece with a high straight baffle dropping off into a large round chamber, will play at a higher pitch than a mouthpiece with a low, short baffle and a medium chamber.  In other words, all other factors being equal, volume distribution can vary pitch almost as much as volume or length.  The tip of the reed is a compression anti--node.  Widening the bore closer to the tip will lower the pitch more than widening the bore in the back of the chamber.  So mouthpiece design is also a factor.

John,

As with the recent case of waveform modulation, which you claimed was impossible, (and which never got a direct response), you are wrong.

Here is my theoretical proof, Part I, which I invite anyone to show invalid.  

Benade's Equivalent Mouthpiece Volume test involves
 coupling a soprano saxophone mouthpiece to a cylindrical tube.   As such, the simple instrument assumes the acoustical characteristics of a closed cylindrical tube - similar to a clarinet.  

The length of a closed cylindrical tube
 constitutes 1/4
 the wavelength of the tube's fundamental resonance.  During the first length of the tube, the reed is open and the wave is a compression wave.  The wave reflects at the open end as a rarefaction and returns toward the reed in it's 2nd length, during which, the reed is still open.  The wave reflects at the reed as a rarefaction, causing the reed to close, and travels the 3rd length of the tube, still a rarefaction.  It reflects at the open end again as a compression wave, and makes it's 4th tube length journey in that state.  The reed is still closed.  When the wave reflects at the reed as a compression wave at the end of the 4th length, the reed opens, and the entire cycle repeats.  

So, during Benade's EMV procedure,  the reed is closed for 1/2 of each wavelength, where the wave travels 2 complete lengths of the tube, and the "volume used" of the mouthpiece is fixed (for the sake of easy comprehension, I
 will give it a volume of 100units.).  Every acoustical characteristic of those 2 tube-length trips, resonance alignment, actual 1/2 wavelength, etc., is a result of the cylindrical tube resonances coupled with that fixed mouthpiece chamber volume of 100 units.

For the other 1/2 wavelength, or 2 tube-length trips of the wave, the reed is open, and the exact volume of the mouthpiece + reed + vocal tract is an unknown "X".  Every acoustical characteristic of those 2 tube-length trips, resonance 
alignment, actual 1/2 wavelength, etc., is a result of the cylindrical 
tube resonances coupled with that unknown "X" mouthpiece volume.

We will arbitrarily take John's initial Benade EMV test results of +28% for our end result here, since it was accurate for his initial test, and will suffice for our purposes.

We can represent all this information in a nice simple mathematical formula:

100 units/half wavelength + X units/half wavelength = 128 units EMV

If we multiply both sides by 2, we get:

100 units + X units = 256 units MV

Then subtract 100 units from both sides:

X units = 156 units MV

The actual mouthpiece + reed + vocal tract volume during the "reed open" phase of the cycle is 156 units.  Benade's procedure results in an averaged actual cavity volume of 128 units, which is more precisely seen as a functional
 volume of 128 units +/- 28 units.

Let's discuss this much for now.  Part II will deal with this kind of averaging and how it applies to the saxophone

        Thanks for that observation Keith.  My comment was taken straight from    Benade.  I may be wrong but my understanding at this point in time is    reflected in these statements.

1.  The "effective" volume of the    mouthpiece is the composite of its physical geometric volume beyond the neck    and the interrelated effects of the motion of the reed, the player's    embouchure, and oral cavity.

2.  This "effective" volume acts as    the surrogate for the missing cone.

3.  This "effective" volume    determines the pitch that is sounded by defining the length of the missing    cone to its apex.

4.  The "effective" volume can be made smaller    by:
   
a.  pushing the mouthpiece farther onto the      cork
b.  taking less mouthpiece into the mouth
c.  using a      harder reed
d.  biting down more on the reed
e.  using a      mouthpiece with a smaller chamber in the same position on the    cork

5.  The "effective" volume can be made larger    by:
   a.  pulling the mouthpiece farther off the cork
b.       taking more mouthpiece into the mouth
c.  using a softer      reed
d.  using a more relaxed embouchure
e.  using a      mouthpiece with a larger chamber  in the same position on the      cork

6.  When the "effective" volume is less than the    volume required by the missing cone
   a.  the instrument plays sharp
b.  the octaves are      too wide making notes sharper in the second octave
c.  the cause is      one or more of the actions described in statement #4.
d.  the      solution is one or more of the actions described in statement    #5

7.  When the "effective" volume is more than the    volume required by the missing cone
   a.  the instrument plays flat
b.  the octaves are      too narrow making notes flatter in the second octave
c.  the cause      is one or more of the actions described in statement #5
d.  the      solution is one or more of the actions described in statement    #4

This is how I see it.  Other's mileage may    vary.
