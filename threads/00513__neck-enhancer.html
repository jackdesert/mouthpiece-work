FROM: lancelotburt (MartinMods)
SUBJECT: Re: Neck enhancer
Since I matched the volume/frs of my mouthpiece/neck combination to my horn so well, I would really hate to screw all that all up by reducing the truncation ratio with a tapered tube stuck in the end of the neck.  I'm still amazed and thrilled at the rock-solid, ideal intonation, effortless response, and defined tonal center that just 5 minutes of simple adjustment gave my set-up, on every mouthpiece/horn combo I have.

I suppose if I was still wallowing around in the mouthpiece mismatch mire that so many saxophone owners are stuck in, that I might be interested in trying something that made my horn play/sound a little different, if only just for a change.  

Reducing the truncation ratio should improve intonation, provided the mouthpiece is properly matched (smaller), though probably at the cost of some tonal flexibility.

The standing wave pressure nodes exist because of the formation of the regime.  If anything causes any of the resonances contributing to a stable regime to change in amplitude or frequency, the entire regime will shift to the fundamental frequency which provides maximum energy.  All of the pressure nodes will shift position, if only a micron.

SUBJECT: Re: Neck enhancer
I think you are mistaken on that, John.  Perhaps you need to review the old postings.  I am and always have been, perfectly content with the constricted neck opening, seeing as how it is an essential element of any reed driven, conical instrument.  

Perhaps you confuse this neck constriction idea with my concern regarding the design parameters of the purely mechanical solution for attaching the mouthpiece to the instrument - the diameter of a piece of cork - determining the acoustical design parameters of the mouthpiece - the diameter of the throat.

To your question: As you have made at least as big of a deal out of the importance of the two requirements of any mouthpiece, volume and frs, surely you are equally as aware as I am, that changing the taper of the neck opening affects frs directly, and thus the tuning characteristics of the mouthpiece/horn combination.

Nederveen:  Definitely, just as changing the taper on the other side of the opening changes frs.

Offset: It is conceivable, in a case where the mouthpiece chamber is too large, and frs is characteristically too high, that using a neck enhancer would alter volume and frs exactly as needed, and to the exact degree, to match the mouthpiece optimally to the horn.  The chances of that are very small.  Otherwise, a similar mouthpiece/horn relationship could be improved or made worse, depending on the degree of the initial volume/frs imbalance.

A mouthpiece/horn relationship that was a good match or on the "too small" side of perfect, would only get worse.

SUBJECT: Re: Neck enhancer
It's not that straight forward Toby, unless you adjust your mouthpiece to keep volume and frs matched perfectly.  As soon as frs is off, you start compromising volume (by placement) in order to get the best over-all tuning.  

From the pictures I've seen of the enhancer, the part that protrudes into the throat, in front of the o-ring seal (about 1/4" looks like) does not fit against the mouthpiece wall.  So you get a 1/4" deep, very thin gap, the full circumference of the tube.  A thin gap traps a lot more energy than a wide gap in a high pressure area, so look to lose a few db of dynamic range and power, at least as much as was gained by eliminating the sudden diameter step.  LOL.

FROM: lancelotburt (MartinMods)
SUBJECT: Re: FRS - How Low Does It Go
I was discussing with Toby on another forum, the question: How low in the saxophone's pitch range does the effect of frs (the cavity resonance) go?

I came up with the answer I think.  Feel free to disagree.  

All the way to low Bb.  As Toby observed, the cavity resonance affects the shorter wavelength resonances, from say A2 up, so those notes will be the most sensitive to frs adjustments.  Those same resonances however, form mode 2, mode 3, and mode 4 (when applicable) of the longer wavelength fundamentals, below A2.  When the short wavelengths' peak frequencies are lowered, it forces the formation of the long wavelength fundamental's regime at a lower, off-peak frequency.  

What does that mean?  Frs affects the pitch center of the entire instrument, even if your volume is correct.

SUBJECT: Re: FRS - How Low Does It Go
So my turn to jump in here: The tube itself has its resonance structure.  Theoretically, if the tube is complete to the tip, that resonance  structure has integra, harmonic partials for any given tube length, and  that will not change either due to tube length or cone angle.

Now  cut the tip off and the partials are no longer harmonic; the modes are  stretched. All notes are somewhat sharpened, but higher modes are  sharpened more than lower modes, and short tube notes are sharpened more  than long tube notes. Also, as cone angle increases, the notes are  sharpened
 more, in the ways noted just above.

So now we need to  try to recreate the resonance structure of the missing conic tip with  the mouthpiece. Ideally, we would want to reproduce all the impedances  of the missing tip, not just the first couple, but this is not possible  since the shape is totally different. As Fletcher says, it is
 probably  not possible to reproduce more than the first two impedances and still  have a functioning mpc shape (given that the reed is a given size).

To  first step is to pull the modes into some sort of alignment, and this  is done, as we know, by matching the volume of the mpc with that of the  missing cone tip. 

The second match is that of frs. This means,  AFAIK, that the first resonance of the mpc matches the first resonance  of the missing conic tip.

Now think a minute; what is the  resonance of the conic tip? The tip is a complete cone, albeit very  short. That little cone has a resonance that has the wavelength twice as  long as the tube itself. I'm not sure exactly what the length of the  missing tip is on an alto, but I'm guessing that it
 is about 25 cm. That  means the tip would "play" a note with a 50 cm wavelength. A2 on alto  is concert C5, with a wavelength about 66 cm long.

What is the  resonance of the mpc? Well, obviously it is the resonance of the part of  the mpc that replaces the tip. That means all the volume (under the  reed and inside the mpc walls) as it is seen by the rest of the cone,  and that means with the exit diameter of the end of the neck.. That is 
 the pure case, as it were.

Of course you could go farther and say  that the frs of the mpc plus the first inch of the neck should be that  of the missing conic tip plus the first inch of neck. Or you could say  that the frs of the mpc plus the whole neck should be equal to the that  of a complete cone to a point where the
 exit diameter of the cone is  equal to the exit diameter of the neck.. 

You could even say that  the frs of the mpc plus the whole tube to the end of the bell should  equal the frs of a complete cone that has the big end the diameter of  the bell. 

The point is that at any given cone exit diameter, the  combination of the mpc plus whatever amount of cone gets you to that  diameter should be the same as a complete cone of that exit diameter.

But  the fact is, that the longer the actual amount of cone you include, the  less a mismatch of the the mpc to the missing cone tip matters, since  it becomes progressively a smaller and smaller proportion of the total  tube length. Remember that the smaller the truncation ratio, the less  the
 modes are affected by a truncation.

Obviously if the frs of  any length of cone + mpc does not match the frs of a complete cone of  the same exit diameter, there is going to be some effect on the  intonation, and just as obviously, the $64,000 question is at what point  that really becomes a problem. Lance is clearly right that a
 mismatch  must have some effect at some level for every tube length, but from what  I have read, a frs mismatch is only going to start being a problem for  notes that have around the same frequency as the frs frequency.

So this brings up an interesting point, since frs is relative to tube length as described.

Let's  say that we measure the "exxtreme frs" case, that of simply the mpc at  the neck opening. And let's say that, for the sake of argument, the frs  is quite low. What we can expect is to find notes at around that  frequency to be pulled flat. But just how much are notes lower than this 
 affected?  How about D2, for instance? 

Well, lets look at it  like this: Take a complete tube and cut it off so that the natural  resonance is that of D2. D2 is equal to concert F4. Concert F4 has a  wavelength of .974 M, so we are looking at a tube of just under half a  meter. That means that if the truncation of an alto is just about
 a  quarter of a meter, we have a mpc mounted on a tube that is another  quarter of a meter long--practically speaking just around where the palm  D tonehole is.

Because there is a fair amount of tube attached  to the mpc to act as a waveguide, the frs is not going to be anywhere  near as far off for the mpc plus tube as for the mpc alone. Since frs is  not anywhere near as flat for mpc + tube for the given note as for mpc  alone for its given note we can
 expect an frs mismatch for the mpc to be  progressively less as we add tube length to the equation.

I do  not know the math and so I cannot quantify this, but from all I have  read, an frs mismatch does not become a serious issue until the note  wavelength is around the same as the wavelength of the frs, which then  starts to exert a "pull" on the note, since it is the primary sounding 
 resonance which is being affected. 

Just as with mode stretching,  if the primary resonance is strong, that is the resonance that is going  to determine the sounding frequency for the most part, even if higher  resonances are out of line. Mode locking will ensure that the partials  are harmonic, and even if the resonances are fairly
 far off, they won't  be able to exert much influence on the regime of oscillation unless they  are either very strong or the primary resonance is weak. What will  happen is that they just won't contribute as much energy to the final  sound in their "place". 

As I explained on SOTW, this is somewhat  like a head cork adjustment in a Boehm flute, which is also a resonance  that affects playing resonances in its neighborhood. You can be quite  far out with a head cork adjustment and it still won't much affect the  first two octaves; its influence is felt
 almost exclusively in the third  octave, unless it is so far out that the third octave is mostly  unplayable.

My comparatively "simple" understanding is this:

Benade listed two requirements for a conical woodwind instrument to "play properly":

-"The equivalent volume of the reed cavity [mouthpiece] added to the mechanical volume of its staple (bocal or neck) must closely match the volume of the missing part of the cone".

-". . . matching Frs for the reed system to the to the first mode natural frequency of the cone apex itself. . . . the [instrument] will then "see" an object at its upper end whose acoustical behavior is quite similar to that of the missing apical cone".

The confusing part seems to come when Benade (p.471)discusses why it is so important that oboists and bassoonists must be so careful to get the right combination of reed [and staple]dimensions (volume of missing cone) and cane stiffness (Frs of reed and staple). A reed that is too stiff will play too high with the same interior volume.  A reed that is too soft will play too flat with the same interior volume.  

In reference to double reed players finding the correct reed stiffness and volume requirement he writes:

" . . . one must simultaneously meet two requirements:

(1) A low frequency requirement on the total equivalent volume of the reed cavity and constricted tube (e.g., the staple, bocal, or neck) associated with it.

(2) A higher frequency requirement that the joint playing frequency Frs of these two objects be properly related to the proportions of the air column. [In other words the frequency of the reed/staple, mouthpiece/neck must match that of the natural resonant frequency of the missing apical cone).

What happens when the volume of the mouthpiece and neck is correct but the player plays too high on the pitch?  The instrument sounds sharp and the player pulls the mouthpiece out farther creating a mismatch of missing cone volume. What happens when the reed is too soft and the player plays too low on the pitch with the correct starting volume?  The instrument sounds flat hence the player pushes the mouthpiece on too far creating too small a volume.  This results in octaves that are too narrow.

Dalmont in his tuning study says this(quoting from Benade):

For a conical woodwind instrument to work properly, the equivalent volume of the reed . . . must closely match the volume of the missing part of the cone. The expression `work properly' is to be [understood] as a minimal inharmonicity of the resonance frequencies.

 frequencies around it, it has to be excited.

In terms of the frs question, I think it is interesting to look at Scavone's thesis. The part in question is his development of tranmission line models of two mpc designs starting on pg 82. In short, he made models of a short fat and a long thin alto mpc and tested their effects on an idealized
 bore. 

A couple if things are important to note here. First, the volumes were carefully matched to the volume of the missing conic tip. Second, the resonance frequency--analogous to frs--was not matched. In fact one would expect the frs of a short fat and long thin mpc to be different, and it is
 therefore rather interesting how this difference would or would not affect the tuning.

"While the narrow mouthpiece provides better normal mode tuning for shorter air column lengths, the tuning effect of these different designs seems negligible."

"It is likely that the mouthpiece geometries could be modified to produce better resonance alignment, though the purpose of this analysis was simply to determine any significant differences between the two designs."

So what we have is a large change in mpc geometry while maintaining a correct inner volume, minus the variables such as reed, lay and embouchure that Lance cannot control for in his experiments. And the result is that the tuning effect of the two designs is negligible.

        Just posted to SOTW a link to a study by Wolfe et al. about the effect of stopper position in tuning on flutes. The "dead space" between embouchure hole and stopper is very important to tuning, forming a sort of Helmholtz resonator which affects all tunings modes somewhat, but especially
 those notes in its neighborhood. Sound familiar? It is largely analogous to the famous frs of which we speak. The paper is interesting largely because it shows the false simplicity which Lance takes for gospel. 

According to Lance, as far as I understand him, if frs lowers modes around its frequency, those modes--if strong--would pull down the frequency of modes below them, in forming the most economical and efficient regime of oscillation.

This is, of course, true to some extent. However what Lance fails to take into account is the fact that--as presented in this paper--the situation is considerably more complex than this, since to (simplistically speaking) get that resonance going in a strong way, in which it can pull down the
 frequencies around it, it has to be excited. 

This is somewhat analogous to sympathetic vibration in strings, as far as I understand it: to get a sympathetic vibration going strongly, you have to excite the passive string with an input of its resonance frequency. A lower mode can excite a passive string only if, and to the extent, that it
 contains the partial of the frequency of the passive string.

So in the case of frs, it appears that lower modes do not excite its resonance in a way that it can affect them.

 not, except in the latter case to point out what doesn't make sense to me at my level of knowledge.

In terms of the frs question, I think it is interesting to look at Scavone's thesis. The part in question is his development of tranmission line models of two mpc designs starting on pg 82. In short, he made models of a short fat and a long thin alto mpc and tested their effects on an idealized
 bore. 

A couple if things are important to note here. First, the volumes were carefully matched to the volume of the missing conic tip. Second, the resonance frequency--analogous to frs--was not matched. In fact one would expect the frs of a short fat and long thin mpc to be different, and it is
 therefore rather interesting how this difference would or would not affect the tuning.

Third, there are neither reed effects nor embouchure variables to worry about in this test: what we have is the pure effect solely of a difference in mpc geometry (and hence only frs, as it were, since the volumes were identical).

Now have a look on pg 84 at figs 2.17 and 2.18. The first is an impedance plot of the two mpcs, If you look closely, you will see minimal, if any, frequency shift in the first 10 peaks at least; in fact, only when we get around the 15th peak do we start seeing a slight frequency shift--although
 there is a fair difference in peak amplitudes in the higher frequencies.

Looking at fig 2.18, we can see a slight difference in normalized partial ratios; with the narrow design being slightly better at short tube lengths.

Scavone's comment sums up the difference between these quite different designs:

"While the narrow mouthpiece provides better normal mode tuning for shorter air column lengths, the tuning effect of these different designs seems negligible."

He continues:

"It is likely that the mouthpiece geometries could be modified to produce better resonance alignment, though the purpose of this analysis was simply to determine any significant differences between the two designs."

So what we have is a large change in mpc geometry while maintaining a correct inner volume, minus the variables such as reed, lay and embouchure that Lance cannot control for in his experiments. And the result is that the tuning effect of the two designs is negligible.

This, in a nutshell, is why I am completely skeptical about Lance's claims.
