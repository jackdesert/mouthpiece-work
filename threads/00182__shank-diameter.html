FROM: saxgourmet (STEVE GOODSON)
SUBJECT: Shank Diameter
Those of us who own multiple mouthpieces all know that there is a  
significant variation between brands in the diameter of the shank,  
resulting in poor fit for some on the neck and the lack of  
interchangeability without applying teflon tape or paper as a spacer.  
Even though this variation is slight, it is sufficient to cause  
problems. It is most unlikely that mouthpiece manufacturers will ever  
agree upon a standard diameter UNLESS some legitimate reason can be  
given to do so.

I got a complaint this morning from a customer who had purchased one  
of our mouthpieces and found that the shank diameter was larger than  
the mouthpieces he had previously used. I have also gotten complaints  
in the past that our mouthpieces were SMALLER than the mouthpiece the  
customer was using! This set me to thinking: is there an acoustic  
reason that one size is better than another? I can find nothing in my  
files which addresses this issue.

Chamber size I understand. I'm speaking ONLY about the diameter of the  
shank itself with regard to the fit on the neck cork.

FROM: lancelotburt (MartinMods)
SUBJECT: Re: Shank Diameter
We went over this some time ago, about how silly it is that the shape and size of the rear of the mouthpiece chamber is determined by a piece of cork, which solves a purely mechanical problem, instead of acoustical considerations having priority in the design.  

It all depends on how you want the volume distributed.  I believe that older Links had a smaller throat diameter so the mouthpiece could be wider at the tip and the side walls more rounded, resulting in a more spread, variable sound.

I read somewhere that the real reason for the Conn Micro Tuner was because the designers felt that keeping the throat at neck opening diameter, up to the actual rounded chamber, had distinct acoustical advantages.

The Conn micro-tuner actually lengthened the neck and therefore the saxophone much like a brass instrument tuning slide.  In so doing it slightly altered the degree of truncation, and more importantly the volume of the "missing cone".  At one time I thought that the microtuner was a perfect way to match the volume of the missing cone with the mouthpiece and then "fine tune" the Frs with the tuner until I realized that each time the length of the neck changes, so does the missing cone volume.

On the older Otto Links, wouldn't just moving the mouthpiece a bit farther onto the neck compensate for the added chamber volume closer to the reed?  Common sense tells me that would be the case.

I have found that the difference between a loose and well-fitting mouthpiece shank to be .010" on the diameter.  If the change occurs only on the shank part that covers the cork, there will be no geometry change inside the part of the mouthpiece that participates in the acoustics.  Evan if it does go into the chamber, its only .010".  The difference from reed-to-reed will be larger.  

Some say a tighter fit on the cork plays better.  I just think if it is loose enough that it leaks or wiggles, that can be a problem.

FROM: saxgourmet (STEVE GOODSON)
SUBJECT: Re: Shank Diameter
We believe that more mass at the shank end increases response....hence  
we make them a bit longer than some other brands.....this also helps  
reduce wiggling, of course. We have no evidence other than anecdotal  
reports and the observations of our play testing posse to support the  
belief in increased mass....I know that many brass manufacturers have  
employed this principle for some time. On a couple of our models, we  
increased the mass on the shank somewhat significantly, and believe  
this made a significant difference in the performance of those  
particular designs. If there are any studies which support or refute  
this contention, I do not have them in my library.

We also contend that increasing the overall mass of the mouthpiece  
(not just the shank) improves response, and have done considerable  
experimenting with different materials (primarily various brass  
alloys) and wall thickness. I strongly disagree with the contention  
that material doesn't matter. Again, our evidence is anecdotal and  
based on extensive play testing. Our vendor who supplies our brass (a  
Japanese company) provided us with billets of different compositions,  
and we found that reducing the zinc content in the alloy was  
beneficial in that it provided a larger spectrum of overtones and gave  
a richer, more complex sound. There seemed to be a point of  
diminishing return on this reduction, and we selected an alloy that we  
thought gave the sound we wanted.

The same principle appears to apply to non-metallic mouthpieces as  
well. We have found that for some applications a rubber based  
composite is more responsive.

"Make the shank slightly larger in diameter, push in the mpc a fraction more. Everybody is happy and the mpc fits on the cork."

If the mouthpiece volume becomes too large due to shank diameter, pushing in to reduce the volume will shorten the length, and the registers will be out of tune.  A smaller shank reduces the volume to the correct amount, and enables the wider, larger chamber up front, without shortening the mouthpiece, and maintains the correct frs.

state that the vintage otto link is a superior mouthpiece, the vintage 
otto link has a narrower shank, therefore the narrow shank makes it a 
superior mouthpiece.  There are far too many variables inside a 
mouthpiece for this to be stated with any real certainty.  Of course one
 can "believe" anything they like, so long as they don't state it as 
though it were a proven fact.

Steve,

When I talked to Joe Wolfe, the acoustic scientist, about this he said that while the scientific community knows that there is a strong belief among players and makers about the effect of material and weight, none of them (including some very accomplished players) can find any physical explanation
 for why it should be so, nor are able to demonstrate it in blind tests. I tend to believe Coltman's explanation, in talking about his experiment:

"...the musician cannot, under normal playing circumstances, dissociate his personal preferences and prejudices from the question at hand. In the case of the three 'flutes' I constructed, nearly every player who picked them up and tried them had a preference for one or the other. Often he would
 describe his impressions--the wooden flute has a 'fuller' tone, the silver one 'projects' much better, etc. He was then usually baffle to find that he could not identify any of the instruments under the 'blindfold' conditions I described. The plain facts are that his judgment is influenced by
 preconceived notions and mental associations of tone quality with other properties of the material. This is a normal human reaction, intensified in the case of those trained to incorporate feeling into their art, and to whom the instrument becomes, in effect, an extension of their own body and
 personality. I do not belittle this attitude; it is, I believe, a desirable condition for the achievement of the fullest artistic expression. It is just not suited for answering narrow, objective questions like the one I posed..."

The ONLY way to know what is really going on is to limit the variables in play and tightly control conditions, so that the foregoing tendencies are eliminated. It doesn't make a whiff of difference if the player is a rank amateur of the greatest virtuoso, the same human fallibilities pertain, and
 perhaps even more so in the more accomplished player. 

I'm not saying that you should spend time and money proving or disproving the point. If you are happy and the players are happy then at the end of the day it really doesn't matter. I do, however, feel honor-bound to present the scientific-consensual opinion whenever anyone makes statements about
 materials that haven't been adequately tested. Just for the record...

well. We have found that for some applications a rubber based 
composite is more responsive.

It is all a matter of degree. To what degree does a change of 1/100" diameter in the shank change the Frs, and to what degree does this mistune the upper partials? To what degree does making the back of the chamber smaller to compensate for a slightly larger shank diameter change the sound? To
