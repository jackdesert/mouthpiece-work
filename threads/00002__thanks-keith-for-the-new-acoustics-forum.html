It is essential to realize that these adjustments (at least as far as my application of them is concerned) are for advanced players only with acoustically superior instruments, and for each individual player and instrument exclusively.  An advanced player knows his embouchure, his horn, and his setup.  He knows how to make his reeds as consistent as possible.  He knows exactly where his "center of gravity" is on his instrument.  From that point, it is entirely possible to get very accurate measurements of the original setup and to compare them to the theoretically optimal for that player/horn/mouthpiece combination, and make some comparison trials.  

"You will notice that Benade does not say to try to calculate the internal volume or

Frs to the nth degree."

He also never described how to design or adjust a mouthpiece facing to the .00X" tolerance, but here you see a bunch of guys talking about it and doing it. 

He did say that saxophone players should be as concerned as oboists regarding cavity volume and frs, so you have to take that as meaning a lot more can be done than we are currently doing.   The fact is, all his examples use the oboe, and it's just not that easy to measure the effective cavity of an oboe since it is the way the reed vibrates that determines it.  He does get pretty specific and Hertzy, when describing how to align the modes of a certain note, in order for it to respond, tune, and sound better.  

When you see how shifting f2 or f3 of low B, just a few cents, on the alto, can make the difference between a low B that cracks and one that doesn't, then you'll appreciate why one would be concerned about a 34 cent discrepancy between the measured and the actual mouthpiece + neck frs.  The 3rd tenor player in the High School band, using a Yamaha 62 tenor, won't notice it, but an advanced player on a great horn will.

In order to understand and appreciate this stuff completely, you have to be an advanced player and you must have at least once, played on an acoustically great vintage horn with a great setup. If you aren't and you haven't, it's like talking about being a great cook, when you have never eaten anything other than fast food.

SUBJECT: Re: Tolerances
"Why does it have to be an "acoustically great vintage horn"? "

Because the more optimally aligned the resonances of a saxophone are to start with, the more noticeable the effect of small acoustical corrections become.  A bad horn may be playable if you damp a good % of the resonances (no resonators).  A so-so horn may become good by making alignment adjustments, but it will won't become better than that.  A good horn may become very good, but never great.  An advanced player will notice that there is always something about all these horns that holds him back as an artist.

A great horn seems to have no limits and becomes what the player wants.  It challenges the player to do more, rather than hold him back.

Saxophones are no different than violins in this respect.

As far a vintage is concerned - IMO, modern horns are to varying degrees acoustically hobbled in that their design sacrifices tonal richness, depth, and responsiveness for easy playability and even tempered tuning.   Something has to be compromised.  If we make the horns easy to blow and easy to tune, we sacrifice some sound but more of the masses will 
be able to play them good enough to enjoy it without too much pain, but an advanced player will quickly discover the tonal limits that he can't go beyond, on such a horn.

I think that two things are important to remember when discussing "vintage" saxophones.  

-One is that a well traveled saxophone can be "great" simply because like a pair of old shoes the (skilled)player is familiar and comfortable with how it sounds, feels, and responds. 

-The second thing that is important is that the "characteristic" saxophone sound in the 20's, 30's, and 40's was much different than it is today.  The saxes and mouthpieces of that era were designed to achieve that concept of sound---especially before the French invasion with their Selmers.

I have mixed feelings about players today who play their Conn 10Ms with high baffle mouthpieces and pimp them out with oversize resos to sound like Michael Brecker or Tom Scott.  There are modern saxes all over the place that were designed with that concept of sound in mind that are much better suited to that style of playing.  I think a Conn 10M should sound like Hawkins playing "Body and Soul".

I have played several Mark VI altos, 3 SBA altos, and about a dozen Mark VI tenors in my career.  Of those:

* 2 tenors, belonging to other players, were great horns
* about 1/2 the others, one of which I own, are very good
* one of the Mark VI altos was great, or very very good (it's not mine)
* one of the SBA altos was great; I own this one

I have also played several Yanagisawa saxophones at a couple of shows (Mel Martin, who endorses these horns, is a good friend of mine so he helped guide me to the good ones.)  One of the sopranos I tried was great or very good, the others merely good.  One of the tenors I tried was great or very good, rivaling the great Mark VIs I have tried.  This one had an aftermarket Yanagisawa solid silver neck.

I therefore doubt the validity of the statement that vintage horns are innately superior.  A great horn is a great horn, and great examples can be found, with about the same degree of regularity, in both vintage and modern instruments.

BTW, when I say "great" I mean a horn that:

* has even response
* is in tune
* most important, has a "singing" tone

I think the first two qualifications are a result of good design, manufacturing and set up; the last is more intangible.  The fact that the "great" modern horn had an aftermarket neck leads me to believe that the acoustic balance of a horn is greatly influenced by this piece of the saxophone, and attention paid here will give good results with any decent instrument.  There are many examples of famous artists who use alternate necks, and I personally know many saxophonists who do the same (Mel among them).  

Additionally, there are countless working pros who use "modern" instruments; if they were sonically inferior, this would not be the case.  

- Steve Keller

OK here I am :)

In essence I agree. But the fact that the effective volume is to a significant extent dynamically determined means that static measurements are of limited value. Further, there are reed parameters that have influence but which cannot be measured accurately. You might remember that Nederveen talks about the fact that double reeds do not have an internal volume anywhere near that of the missing cone, but that the oboe,for example, still manages to play in tune due to compensating effect of the reeds themselves. 

Changing that small double-reed volume does affect the modes, but a simple measure of internal volume would be misleading. Sax reeds likewise lower all the impedances in ways that are not direcly predictable formulaicly. You can't just plug in a fixed reed constant.

The point is not that we shouldn't try to measure the parameters that we can and try to correlate the results with observed behavior, but trying to create a formula to accurately predict acoustic behavior from a limits number of relevant parameters is tantamount to chasing a chimera.

You make some good points Toby.  First, let me point out to you that
musicians are making very precise and consistent embouchure settings
every time they tune their instrument.  They blow with their "centered"
embouchure and airstream which incorporates muscle memory developed from
thousands of hours of playing and compare that to the fixed pitch of the
tuner.  If they match isn't perfect, then move their tuning device,
sometimes a very minor amount to adjust the length of the instrument to
the temperature of the room or hall at that time.

If the musician did not have this embouchure sensitivity, he could not
accurately tune the instrument to begin with.  Of course even the best
musician would be hard pressed to pick up the instrument and in 20
trials produce right from the start, the exact same frequency down the
the last hertz.  This is why I believe the artificial embouchure device
(if it works) will be a useful tool in these studies.

It is true that absolute accuracy is not possible in either calculating
the length and volume of the missing cone or calculating the natural
resonant frequency  of the length of the neck + the missing cone + end
correction.  However, if a good approximation of those values can be
determined, then comparing that calculated frequency to the actual
played frequency of the mouthpiece plus neck can give a good idea of
whether or not the mouthpiece in that position on the cork is acting as
an effective substitution for the missing cone.

It is my understanding that when the mouthpiece  is in the position on
the neck to have its effective volume match that of the missing cone,
then

-the instrument tunes to AD0
-the long tube low B and Bb along with their 2nd partials are in tune
-the low F and middle F octaves are in tune
-the short tube notes in the second register are close to being in tune
(after all it is a saxophone) [:)]

This indicates that both of Benade's requirements have been met and the
mouthpiece has produced a good match with the instrument.  Of course
this assumes a degree of skill on the part of the player, reed that is
working well, and a constant, moderate temperature.   I don't believe
that the fact that it is not possible to measure any of these variables
to perfection should prevent one from even trying to do these types of
studies.   If the "perfect" is allowed to be the enemy of the "good"
then there would be no progress at all in understanding this area of
acoustics a little better.  N'est pas?

A careful reading of my missing cone study will show that:

- once the length and volume of the missing cone is calculated,

-and the effective volume of the mouthpiece is measured,

-then the empirical trials of playing the mouthpiece on the neck are
done to see if the played frequency of the mouthpiece on the neck
matches the calculated frequency of the missing cone plus the neck.

To say "empirical analysis does not work"  demonstrates a lack of
understanding of the term empirical.
<http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical>

Artificial embouchures for both brass and woodwind instruments have been
used by acoustic scientists for many years to accurately measure and
quantify  the sound wave produced in different settings without the
variable of the human player.  Even the best human player cannot come
close to the repeatability of a machine when it comes to producing
exactly the same pressure on the reed, and exactly the same volume and
pressure of air in hundreds of trials.   To call that a "worthless idea"
demonstrates a total lack of understanding of how acoustic science  is
conducted.

--I disagree to some extent. Musicians first tune to notes that are harder to lip up or down, usually around the middle of the first register (or between the bottom and the middle). They then dynamically tune as they play, based--as you say--on thousands of hours of practice. This is not so much
 different than a violin player knowing exactly where to put her finger to get a note on a fretless fingerboard, or more exactly, the neighborhood, which is then fine-tuned by extremely quick finger shadings based on feedback from what she hears.
--A sax player, after tuning to a general pitch center, adjusts each note dynamically and almost instantaneously (if he is good) with fine embouchure and breath pressure adjustments to hit the correct pitch as he plays or in response to the pitch of other instruments around him. If this were not
 the case, it would be impossible to play in tune, given the intonational compromises of the instrumental system in its entirety.



If the musician did not have this embouchure sensitivity, he could not accurately tune the instrument to begin with.  Of course even the best musician would be hard pressed to pick up the instrument and in 20 trials produce right from the start, the exact same frequency down the the last hertz.

--In a number of trials, it was found that even professional musicians generally cannot hit pitch to within 10 cents in repeated trials without a reference (this was with flute players). Some were up to 20 cents off. Of course as soon as there is an external pitch to reference to this changes
 completely.

It is true that absolute accuracy is not possible in either calculating the length and volume of the missing cone or calculating the natural resonant frequency  of the length of the neck + the missing cone + end correction.  However, if a good approximation of those values can be determined, then
 comparing that calculated frequency to the actual played frequency of the mouthpiece plus neck can give a good idea of whether or not the mouthpiece in that position on the cork is acting as an effective substitution for the missing cone.
--Agreed. The keywords (and point of contention) here are "good approximation". My own little tests have shown that if the player is in tune in the first register, the mpc is acting as a good approximation. The question then becomes what happens to the second register and above, and if there are
 intonational variations whether the problem lies in the mpc or in the body itself, or some combination of the two. It seems to me that the most valuable study, rather than endless minute calculation of the volume and frs of the mpc and/or neck, would be as follows: 

--Put a mpc on the horn and blow it with your artificial embouchure, choosing one note to tune to. It doesn't matter whether it is exactly like a real player. Just find out--with a given mpc/reed--how the general note-to-note tuning is and what the octave relationships are with a non-changing
 embouchure pressure+breath pressure for a series of notes. 

--I would tune to G1 and then play from bottom to top (insofar as possible using the artificial embouchure. Probably it will not be possible to play the lowest notes with the same setting as for the upper notes). This will give you a good idea about the intonational tendencies of the horn due to
 tone hole placement (the entire first mode from low Bb through C#2), and the octave relationships (D2-F3). A second test would be to detune G1 by a given amount (say +- 20 cents) and again run the notes and record the intonational tendencies across all registers.
--This will immediately show you how changing the mpc volume affects the register relationships, and perhaps intonational trends within registers from long- to short-tube notes.

--Now change the mpc volume and reset the mpc so that it is in tune at G1. Repeat the tests and see if there is an average difference in the mode relationships. This should give you a good idea what real-world effects changing the mpc volume have on intonation after the mpc is reset to play the
 base note (G1) in tune again.

--Personally, it seems to me that this would be a much more fruitful use of your time and skill than trying to figure out volumes and resonant frequencies and match them against a hypothetical missing cone, (especially when you don't even know how to calculate that latter value due to variations
 in the conicity of the sax body/neck).
