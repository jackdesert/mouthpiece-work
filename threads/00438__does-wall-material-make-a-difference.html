SUBJECT: Does wall material make a difference?
It seems to me that I have read somewhere recently that on saxophones, the more zinc in the brass, the brighter the tone, and the more copper in the brass, the darker the tone. Also that silver "resonates more quickly" so the sax responds quicker.

Does anyone have any thoughts on this topic? Can the wall material of a saxophone body and/or neck actually make a perceptible difference in the tone and response, or is this just more marketing hype in order to sell more instruments?

FROM: jbtsax (John)
SUBJECT: Re: Does wall material make a difference?
Since it is the makers and sellers who are making the claims in their
ads that metals/finishes make a difference, it seems to me that they
should be the ones willing to step up to the plate with some data to
back up their claims.

Cannonball Saxophones actually patented the process and concept of
adding weight/mass to specific areas of the saxophone and neck to change
the sound and did in fact include measurements of overtones in trials
with and without the materials added as a part of their patent.

Cannonball Patent
<http://www.google.com/patents?id=fUXIAAAAEBAJ&printsec=abstract&zoom=4&\
source=gbs_overview_r&cad=0#v=onepage&q&f=false>    The charts and
graphs are quite tedious to read and digest, but the figures do show a
greater "harmonicity" of the upper partials with the stones in the keys.
Whether this was unconsciously player induced or a result of the heavier
keys with the stones attached is anyone's guess.

Of course there has been controversy surrounding their results and how
"scientific" their tests really were, and there are differing opinions
about that.  However, the fact is that the Cannonball company was one of
the only manufacturers on record to make an effort to come up with some
data to back up their perceptions of how their saxes played differently
with different different variables in effect.

Whether they are right or wrong, I believe they should at least be given
credit for making the effort.  It would be great if more makers and
designers would make the same effort to put some science behind their
claims IMO.  And you are right Keith, it should not only apply to
saxophone makers and designers, but other instruments as well.

John

Letting manufacturers do their own trials and provide
their own data is putting the fox in charge of the
henhouse. No one with an interest in the results should be
doing the testing, as there are too many ways to skew
data, either consciously or unconsciously. This can and
does happen even in the most careful experiments done by
impartial and experienced researchers, which is why
nothing is released without peer review.

least.

SUBJECT: Re: Does wall material make a difference?
There is an important element that you are overlooking Chuck and that is the interior of the bore.  Small changes in the texture of the surface and the geometry inside the bore of the instrument produce far greater changes in the sound than anything on the outside.

A saxophone in raw brass will have an acoustically different interior surface than one with silver plating or black nickel anodized plating.  The "Brute" finish which is chemically aged brass inside and out will have a different interior than a raw brass or lacquered saxophone.

It is an intellectually fallacious argument to state "If you don't believe A then you must believe B".  You will be the first to know what I actually believe when I actually make that statement, which I have not made yet because all the proof is not in.

The perception of sound through bone conduction is extremely poor, which is one reason that cochlear implants only work near the cochlea.

I did an interesting little informal experiment on my last airplane trip. I have a good pair of noise-cancelling headphones, and I noticed that if I touched an earcup to the airplane wall, the sound of the aircraft was quite vigorously transmitted to my ear by its vibration. Since the negative
 feedback of the acoustic circuit in the phones effectively brought ambient noise down by about 20 dB as perceived through the ears, I thought this might be a good time to find out about just how strong bone conduction really is. First I touched my chin to the wall, wearing headphones. There was a
 barely perceptible difference, probably around 2 dB more. Then I touched my top teeth to the wall and pressed about as hard as I would playing the sax (my seatmate was asleep, luckily). I was surprised to find that there was barely more conduction that way: probably a maximum of 3-4 dB. Touching
 my temple to the wall, though, was quite noticeble, something between 7-10 dB.

This informal experiment leads me to believe that "bone conduction" as a source of feedback in sax playing is way overstated by those grasping at straws to explain perceptual differences that are more cogently explained by observer bias.

SUBJECT: Re: Does wall material make a difference?
For one thing, given the wide variation in tone color that any player can achieve with minimal embouchure variation, it would be very hard to establish a reliable baseline, and of course steady-state notes do not really tell you about response differences in transients.

Only an artificial embouchure would give repeatable results, and even those would require repeated trials to average out small differences in reed and/or "mouth" placement. Real scientists have to know a lot about statistics...

Further, as necks are quite complex geometrically, it would not be so easy to control for measurement variablilty, especially as different metals respond quite differently to various forming processes.

If those problems could be solved, though, good double blind testing using skilled (not necessarily professional) players would certainly be relatively easy to carry out and quite illuminating.

Of course such testing has already been done on a respectable level by Widholm with flutes, and the results led inescapably to the conclusion that materials were not significant in terms of contributing to the final sound. 

Realistically, makers who make claims to the contrary have nothing to gain and everything to lose by trying to verify their results scientifically, since not one experiment in the past 100 years has given anyone any reason to believe that materials or finishes affect woodwind response.

And makers, like politicians, know that if you repeat something enough, it becomes a truth in the minds of all but the most aware people. Aware people are not a large segment of the market...

Oh boy!  We haven't had a good argument for a long time Toby.  This
should be enlightening an illuminating.  I won't quote but will respond
by number to your numbered statements.

1.  The professional performer on a wind instrument who plays the same
instrument with the same "set up" every day of the week 4 to 6 hours per
day in practice, rehearsals, and performance develops an intimacy and
acute sensitivity to the way that instrument plays.  Minute changes to
that instrument are readily perceptible to that performer even though
the passive listener a few feet away can hear no difference whatever.

Your mentioning that I am saying that Gilbert's study is flawed is a
straw man argument to say the least.  Gilbert found that the wall
vibrations of thin walled oval tube can have a measurable effect upon
the sound produced under certain conditions.  Although his study is the
most comprehensive to date discounting the effects of wall vibrations in
woodwinds,  he does leave the door open slightly with this comment.

  "For real orchestral instruments, although the previously described
mechanism is not to be excluded completely for a particular instrument,
similar effects are thus unlikely to be measurable and, even if they
were, would only occur for one or a few notes.

2.  Smith's study is one of the least professional papers that I have
read.  There is a much greater body of research done by Whitehouse that
seems to contradict Smith's findings.   Smith's conclusions relative to
the skilled players he tested is nothing more than "anecdotal" since he
gives no actual statistical charts or data.

3.  Gilbert's study is excellent as far as it goes measuring the sound
from a thin walled brass tube generated by an artificially blown
clarinet mouthpiece.  My point is that no study,  including his can
accurately measure the perception to the highly skilled  player
intimately familiar with the make and model of instrument of different
variables including metals and finishes.

The glaring omission from Smith's trombone bell study and the
Widholm/Lidortner flute studies is the failure to ask each of the
players,  "When you played those two instruments side by side, did they
feel and sound the same or different"?  It is one thing to be able to
"pick" which one is the thin bell, or which one is the platinum flute
and an entirely different thing to recognize that each instrument had a
different feel, response, and aural feedback to the player.

As far as plating and anodized finishes being both inside and out the
saxophone body, I will need to do some more investigation to say for
certain on the Cannonball and Yamaha instruments I have the best access
to.  I do know from taking with techs who do a lot of plating that
successive layers of plating do in fact tend to fill in microscopic gaps
in the surface of the brass---especially copper plating. Whether this is
enough to create an acoustically different surface, I don't know. 
However, I am open to the possibility.

As far as my "friends" at Cannonball who play hundreds of saxes each
year of the identical model in different finishes, all I can say is that
"I believe that they believe that they perceive a difference in the tone
color".    Since I don't have that experience, nor do I play at that
level, I would hesitate to say they are imagining things or being
deliberately deceptive.

I also know that players on the level of Pete Christlieb, Branford
Marsalis, Gerald Albright, and the New Century Saxophone Quartet have
all gone to the Salt Lake City headquarters and spent hours playing
dozens of saxophones and necks in different finishes to select the
instrument that most efficiently produces the "sound" and "response"
that most closely matches their concept.

I have never felt comfortable in the camp of those who have the audacity
to say that those professional woodwind players who are some of the most
intelligent people in the world are "deluding themselves" into the false
impression that when they play two or more nearly identical instruments
in different finishes that they perceive a difference in the response
and tonal color.

I believe that there are aspects of the bioacoustic feedback loop that
are not yet well understood, especially when it comes to woodwinds. 
Anyone who has ever heard themselves on the playback of a good solo
recording knows that the sound the listener hears is markedly different
from the sound "heard" (and felt) by the performer.  I know that I tend
to flip flop and argue both sides of this question.  That simply means
that I don't have all the answers, and that I believe there is a lot
more to learn on this fascinating topic.

I *believe* that the shape and finish of the neck is critical to saxophone sound.  This is because I have played necks that play well on lots of different horns; the neck is the only thing that changes, and the horn plays better, or worse, depending.

Hi John,

Nothing new in that study. It does appear that there are significant vibrations caused by the beating of the reed against the mpc, and if you remember Nederveen even postulates that vibrations of the neck caused by the beating reed might affect the player's embouchure and possibly cause a maximum
 of 3 dB difference from what it would be in the absence of such vibrations.

However it is important to remember that the vibrational differences between different materials is small compared to "no vibration at all", so it would not be anywhere near 3 dB. 

Note also that in this study the main cause of vibration was the brass bell. We all know that brass bells, being wide and flared as they are, relatively thin and the only exit for all the sound power of the instrument (which is considerably stronger than that of any woodwind, by an order of
 magnitude or so (at least in the case of trombone)), are going to have a significant effect on the sound. 

There is no significant vibration in the puny flare of the sax bell, which only really comes into play anyway in the bottom couple of bell notes.

You should also note that the brass bell can actually couple and resonate at playing frequencies (one of the three conditions expressed in that paper), whereas this does not happen in the sax at all. This is an important, actually vital, condition to having enough vibration to affect anything.

Please also remember that in the Gilbert study, the beating reed did not seem to create any vibrations that materially affected the sound, and even when the resonances of the tube were brought into "ovalling" range by making it elliptical, those quite large vibrations only affected a few notes.

Extrapolating from all this data, it seems reasonable to conclude that even if the beating reed does introduce some vibration, it would be insignificant, or at worst it would have a very slight effect on a few notes. Gilbert calls even this "unlikely" in normal musical instruments, although it
 might have some effect in large and thin organ pipes.

Toby,

In particular, I would like to call your attention to this statement
from page 2 of the study.

Moore et al. (2005) has concluded that the most likely reason for the
wall vibrations having an effect is mechanical feedback of vibrations
from the bell to the players' lips through the walls. The
players' lip movements are then affected which then affects the
power spectrum. They also propose that slight changes in the input
impedance at higher frequencies are due to changes in the viscous
boundary layer, which increases with vibration magnitude; they postulate
that it is a combination of both of these two effects. There are three
couplings relevant for transfer of vibrations: (1) between the internal
air and the walls, (2) between the walls and the external air, and (3)
between the players' lips and the walls.

I would also like to clarify my premise in this discussion.  That is
that believe that the wall vibrations of thin walled woodwinds (flutes
and saxophones) may effect the  perception of the highly skilled
performer.  I believe this to be a possibility even though the
measurable effects of the wall vibrations directly affecting the emitted
sound are negligible.

It is also well known that skilled players can alter the timbre of the
sound at will using the embouchure, air stream, and oral cavity.  I
believe it entirely possible that the different  bioacoustic feedback to
the player from a raw brass or plated instrument as opposed to a
lacquered one could affect how they interact with the instrument
resulting in a difference in tone.

Lastly, there is the "self-fulfilling prophecy" aspect which would mean
that a trumpet player who believes silver plated instruments sound
brighter and brassier  will unconsciously play with a brighter and
brassier tone when he plays one.  The question then becomes
philosophical rather than scientific.  Did the silver finish trumpet
sound brighter and brassier in this instance?  Certainly it did?  Did
the finish affect the sound?  Absolutely.  Did the player deliberately
deceive himself or his listeners (BS as some call it)?  I don't think
so, if the player honestly believed that the finish affects the sound.

One simply cannot remove the human element in these discussions and talk
about the physics and acoustics of wind instruments in a vacuum because
the interaction between the human being and the instrument is an
integral  part of the finished product that the listener hears.
