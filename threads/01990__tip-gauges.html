FROM: sjrosner (Jeff Rosner)
SUBJECT: Re: Tip Gauges
I have a Babbitt tip gauge and a number of depth gauges...the Babbitt 
gauge (at least the one that I have) is surprisingly accurate, but 
requires more thought to get a good reproducible measurement. You 
need to make certain that you are reading it at the right place. 
Generally (for a typical tip cut) the gage contacts the tip rail at 
the 'inside' (i.e. towards the chamber) edge of the tip rail. You 
therefore have to read the gage at this point, NOT at the tip...many 
folks make this mistake. The depth gage always picks the highest 
point of the tip rail. I have found pretty good agreement between my 
depth gauges and the wand when I am careful...has a consistent offset 
of ~0.0015. The wand is much faster to use.

It depends on the taper of the wand. My Babbitt wand tapers 
about .001 for each .023 of length. So for an .023 tip rail, the 
error is .001, for an .069, it is .003. Probably not to worry about, 
but enough to require awareness and consistency.

FROM: sjrosner (Jeff Rosner)
SUBJECT: Re: Tip Gauges
I have a theory that has held up to date on these. The tip rail is 
always angled with respect to the table. Most of us (I think) call 
the tip opening the distance from the plane of table to the closest 
point on the tip rail...this is because that is what we measure with 
a wand or a depth gauge. If you measure with a caliper right at the 
tip, however, you always get a larger number. I added a diagram to 
the <Gauging> photo directory to indicate what I am blathering 
about...

jeff

When I see messages like this I just assume the member wants to be deleted and does not know how to do it themselves.  So I delete them.
