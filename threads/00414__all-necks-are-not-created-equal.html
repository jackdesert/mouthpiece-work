SUBJECT: All necks are not created equal
I just recently finished working on a customer's King Super 20 tenor sax
on which he wanted some extra "custom" neck work done.

He wanted to have a neck converted to use O rings instead of a cork, but
did not want to use the neck that came with his Super 20 so he bought a
used Cannonball Fat Neck to do this with.

The tenon on the neck had to be shrunk to fit the receiver on his sax
and the octave key on the neck needed to be extended to work with his
octave mechanism.

Once that was finished, play testing showed that the Cannonball neck
played considerably sharper (15-20 cents) in the upper register than the
"stock" neck---especially on the short tube notes.  I did not check the
palm key notes since they can be tuned somewhat with key heights and
crescents.  The customer was made aware up front that the new neck might
behave differently acoustically, but he insisted on doing the work
anyway in order to find out.  The specs for each neck are as follows.

Item              Fat neck      King Neck
Length                257 mm            250 mm
Volume              84 ml                 81 ml
small end          13.56 mm        12.95 mm
larger end         25.87 mm        25.83 mm
taper                   1.37 degrees   1.47 degrees


The question in my mind is whether somehow increasing the taper of the
Fat Neck by reducing the diameter of the small end would correct the
octave intonation problem.  Would there be any other solution to the
intonation problem created by the different neck?

I have long been a skeptic of "one size fits all" aftermarket necks that
are hyped by some vendors as being an improvement over the stock necks
on many different makes and models.  I would think that there are just
too many different lengths and tapers of necks on existing saxophones
for one aftermarket neck to work on all of them.  I suppose one would
need to measure and compare the specs of many different necks to know
for sure.  That might be something we could do collectively on this
forum, if others are interested.

John

I too have always been skeptical about one-size-fits-all, at least insofar as intonation goes. You might wish to try reducing the inner diameter from about where the cork begins to the end with some sort of putty temporarily and see what that does. That would be the equivalent of "necking in",
 which is said to improve the upper register intonation.

FROM: saxgourmet (STEVE GOODSON)
SUBJECT: Re: All necks are not created equal
I am unfamiliar with the Cannonball Fat Neck, never having seen or  
played one. I would think that the significant difference in the  
easily measured physical dimensions would clearly indicate serious  
potential problems. I have several Super 20 necks, and all of them  
have a very uneven taper (not a straight cone), as do many necks. This  
variation in taper is often a significant part of the design and  
cannot be ignored.

We have found a tremendous variation in this taper among  
manufacturers, really more than I expected. Some are quite similar  
(Selmer and Yamaha, for example) although all have their differences.  
Rarely can you swap the neck from one brand to another and obtain  
perfect results. Length and diameter are important, but taper is  
equally so.

Altering taper "after the fact" is difficult but not impossible. If  
you are starting with a neck which is as dimensionally different as  
you describe, I would suspect it would take an awful lot of trial and  
error work (with the meter running, of course!) to find the optimum  
solution. Even then, the results may not be entirely satisfactory. Our  
shop rarely accepts this type work any more. We do some modification  
of Selmer necks, as over the years we have obtained considerable  
experience with them, but that's now about it.

I've measured many necks, and keep a "reference stash" of most of the  
common ones on hand. When we got into the neck business, we originally  
made necks only for Selmer and compatible Yamaha models. We have now  
expanded into necks for quite a few other brands, and contrary to what  
has been reported on the internet, they are all very different from  
one another.

My suggestion is that you evaluate the taper from not a total point of  
view, but select multiple points for your measurements. I think you  
will see that the neck which gives the best results is rarely a  
straight, even cone.

SUBJECT: Re: All necks are not created equal
We learn as we go along, of course.....the neck design you reference  
in your post was done in 2004, and was produced until 2006. No more  
have been manufactured since then. Yes, it worked very well on many  
different saxophones, better than the original equipment. No, it was  
not and was never intended to be a perfect solution for every horn. We  
never claimed that it was, only that buyers were very happy with the  
excellent results. We sold a lot of them, and all the buyers were very  
happy. I think our approach here might best be likened to that of  
Ponzol or Oleg, who manufacture one basic model which gives improved  
results on multiple instruments.

When we began the manufacture of our own series of necks in 2008,  
after leaving Orpheus Music, we began with essentially a clean sheet  
of paper. ALL of the designs (horns, mouthpieces, and necks) were  
entirely new except for the Saxgourmet Saxello, which is the ONLY  
product carried over (we were very happy with it and it still sells  
very well). We even revised the oils, lubricants, and other products.  
The necks we manufacture today (we currently produce about a dozen  
different versions, with some sub-variations within each version) are  
brand specific, and different from one another. We do not use the "one  
size fits all, just adjust the tenon" approach.  We maintain a  
"reference stash" of examples of many different brands, and yes, they  
vary considerably from one another in length, diameter, taper, and pip  
placement. To the best of my knowledge, we are the only neck  
manufacturer who does this.
